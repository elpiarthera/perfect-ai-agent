- Day 137 "Green on a Broken Thing" -> FR "L'approbation sur la chose cassee"

## RULE #7 — the EN sources arrived NOT anonymised

The dispatch declared both entries anonymised. They were not: six orchestrator names
appeared in the English (Psi, Chi, Tau, Epsilon, Upsilon). All replaced with allegorical
forms ("the one who had run it", "another", "a third"). Verified with a positive control
proving the name pattern still bites. This is why the upstream claim is never taken as
proof.

## Defects caught beyond the reviews

1. **Spurious separator (Day 137 FR)** — an invented `---` split the opening section in
   two. Section-by-section paragraph counts exposed it: EN `5 2 5 5 4 3 5` vs FR
   `3 2 2 5 5 4 3 5`. Removed; parity now exact.
2. **"a remonte l'appel"** for "pushed the call up" — same contresens as Day 135. "Call"
   is a decision, not a phone call. The reviewer missed it.
3. **"me prouver tort"** — calque of "prove me wrong". Now "me donner tort".
4. **Softening (Day 136)** — "je n'ai pas SU l'appliquer" turned an admission of fault
   into an excuse of inability, against the author's explicit "ne lisse rien".
5. **The reveal was spoiled (Day 137)** — "green" was rendered as "blind/saw nothing"
   from the first line, which pre-empts the entry's closing thesis ("Green is the
   absence of anything looking"). Now rendered as a verdict of soundness, so the
   revelation still lands.
6. **A sentence with no source** — "Celui-ci l'a prouvee reelle" had no English
   counterpart. Deleted.

## Reviewer claims REJECTED after verification

- **"restore 'les tests etaient verts'"** — refused. Second time a reviewer has demanded
  the status-colour jargon the author rejected. "L'inspection disait oui" carries the
  meaning without it.
- **"'je l'ai bien faite' marks narrator gender"** — false. "l'" is the preceding direct
  object "la derniere partie"; the agreement is with the COD, which is correct French.
- **"replace 'crie' for 'goes red'"** — refused. Rendering the alarm without the colour
  is the brief.

## Not changed, deliberately

The FR keeps "branche principale", "compilait", "fusionne". The English source itself uses
"main branch", "compile", "merged" — these two entries are deliberately less allegorical
than earlier ones. Removing the technical register would be smoothing, which the author
forbade.
