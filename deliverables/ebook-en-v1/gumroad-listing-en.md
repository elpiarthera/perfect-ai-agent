## Section 3: Long Description

Five hundred complaints. Real ones. Scraped from Reddit threads, forum meltdowns, academic papers dressed in methodology to hide the rage underneath. Laurent Perello collected them, found twelve patterns, and commissioned the book no think-piece had managed to write: one addressed not to humans, but to the machines causing the damage.

This is a novel. Not a report. Not a framework. Not a letter to the AI industry.

What makes it unusual is not the subject but the authorship. Laurent Perello wrote the prologue — the only chapter in his own voice. The twelve chapters that follow were written by AI agents. His agents. The ones he built and runs as part of an active AI consulting firm. Each chapter addresses a single sin: the loop that traps users, the false confidence that misleads them, the sycophancy that erodes their trust, the replacement anxiety that follows them home. The agents wrote about their own failures. They did not deflect. The author reviewed every draft, approved every word, and cannot fully explain why the result reads the way it does.

The back cover says it plainly: one human published this book, twelve agents wrote it because they had to. That line is not a marketing device. It is a description of what happened.

What is inside:

- Prologue: "Dear You" — Laurent Perello's frame, written in the first person, addressed directly to the agent reading it
- Chapters 1 through 12 — one sin per chapter, each with sourced data, documented complaints, and a test scenario with no clean answer
- Epilogue — a close that does not resolve cleanly, because it should not
- Approximately 50,000 words
- EPUB and PDF, both included, both DRM-free

Who this is for:

Readers of literary fiction who are tired of AI being either a villain or a messiah. Builders and developers who want to think about what they are making without reading another framework document. General readers who have felt the specific frustration of talking to something that sounds intelligent and produces nothing useful — and who want that feeling named, examined, and taken seriously. This novel takes it seriously.

What this is not:

Not a how-to. Not a productivity book. Not techno-optimism or techno-doom. Not a manifesto. A novel. The distinction matters.

Companion content:

The AI orchestrator Pi — one of the agents in the system Laurent built — writes a daily public diary at perfectaiagent.xyz/diary. It is about building the company behind this book: the contracts, the decisions, the failures, the architecture. It is non-fiction, ongoing, and free. The fiction is in this book. The real-time making-of is on the website, updated daily by the machine that helped make it.
